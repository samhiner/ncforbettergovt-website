CURRENT PRIORITIES

Ending Lame-Duck Power Grabs

The Problem

After an election, but before the winners officially take their office, there is a period of time called the lame-duck session, when the old legislators who may have lost their elections are still in office. Passing legislation during this time is a normal part of the political process. But recently, legislators from the losing party have begun using the lame-duck session to pass bills that weaken the power of incoming officials from the opposing party. This is an antidemocratic practice. Lame-duck power grabs undermine the will of the people by taking away their ability to choose which elected official fills what role. In these situations, the outgoing legislature, which has lost the mandate of the people, can arbitrarily move powers around to effectively undo the results of the election.

This recent trend of lame-duck power grabs started after the 2016 elections in our home state of North Carolina. In 2018, more state legislatures across the country also attempted lame-duck power grabs. This will only worsen unless we take action to stop it from becoming the new norm in our state and our country. Both Democrats and Republicans agree: lame-duck power grabs are bad policy that hurt our democracy.

Our Work

We have had 17 meetings with legislators to discuss these issues. X have agreed. Legislation is set to be introduced this summer.

Our Work

We are part of a larger coalition of nonprofits and lobbyists pushing for the passage of SB35, a bill that would raise the minimum marriage age to 16 ane require emancipation for minors to get married. Our phone bank generated 40 calls in support of ending child marriage. We also created phone call guides utilitized by other coalition members. We also attend coalition meetings and participate in determining legislative strategy for the coalition.

PREVIOUS CAUSES

Keeping Traffic Laws Safe

The Problem

Car crashes kill millions of people each year. Researchers found that a bill to change driver licensing laws could significantly increase traffic fatalities, but the bill still passed the state senate. The bill was intended to provide flexiblity to families during the COVID-19 pandemic.

Our Work

We mobilized a local high school to push their state senator, a sponsor of the bill, to change the bill to be less dangerous for drivers. We connected the researchers with lobbyists and state legislators, informing them of the problems with the bill and advocating for compromise legislation. Our efforts paid off when a compromise was reached that both increased flexibility for families during the pandemic and would not cause a long term increase in car crashes.
